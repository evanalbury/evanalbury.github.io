Bike Gearbox Design - Second Year University

Developed a gearbox for an EAPC for transmitting power from an electric motor to the rear wheel. Achieved a 2:1 in the Project.

The project began with calculating the required gear ratio to match motor output while complying with UK regulations. We explored multiple concepts — including belt drives, multi-stage gearboxes, and planetary designs — before selecting a compact two-stage gearbox as the most effective solution.

Once the concept was agreed, I focused on detailed design: selecting gear module and thickness, producing CAD models, and running FEA simulations to validate performance. We then manufactured a prototype using laser-cut acrylic and tested it two weeks before the final assessment. During testing, a gear sheared from the shaft at high speed, which taught us the importance of robust load transfer methods. In response, we redesigned the assembly with clamped, bolted joints instead of relying solely on adhesive.

Our final design performed strongly. We scored in the top 10% for material efficiency and fitting time, and achieved a competitive distance result. Although a small oversight (exposed rotating nuts) limited our mark in one category, the project developed my skills in CAD, simulation, design iteration, prototyping, and testing under real-world constraints.

Water Bottle Rocket Launcher - First Year University

Designed and built a launcher for compressed air-powered water bottle rockets. Integrated safety mechanisms, pressure regulation, and conducted launch tests to evaluate trajectory stability and thrust efficiency.

The project began with manufacturing the mechanical launch system using a range of workshop tools, including 3D printing, CNC machining, pillar drills, and guillotines. The initial launcher worked but lacked stability and could only fire rockets straight upwards. To solve this, we designed a stand with rails to guide the launch angle and ground pegs for stability, alongside modifications to the rocket itself.

We refined the launcher design with a plywood frame, a nose cone on the rocket to reduce drag, and fins to shift the centre of pressure rearwards for improved stability. To control flight distance, we combined MATLAB modelling with physical testing, using real-world data to improve our simulation accuracy.

On test day, our model predicted the required pressure and water volume for a 70 m launch. All three attempts landed within 10 m of the target, earning a first-class result. The project developed my skills in CAD, prototyping, MATLAB programming, data analysis, and experimental validation.
